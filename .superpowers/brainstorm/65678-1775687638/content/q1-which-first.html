Where do we start?

Both features are right for 2026. They need to be built in an order. Which do we design first?

A

Conviction Ledger

RAPHI tracks every research output it generates — ML signal direction, SEC trend verdict, sentiment call — then looks back at 30/60/90 days to check what proved true. Over time it builds an accuracy record per signal type and per market regime. The RIA can see: "RAPHI's SEC trend signals have been right 74% of the time at 90 days for large-cap tech."


What it solves: Trust. No AI financial tool today tracks its own accuracy. This is how RAPHI becomes defensible, not just useful.

Depends on: Existing ML signal + SEC data (already built). Adds a hypothesis store + lookback job.

User payoff: Every research output becomes a bet RAPHI is willing to be accountable for.

B

Portfolio Narrative Contradiction

RAPHI looks across all held positions and surfaces when the macro theses are internally incoherent — e.g. "You hold NVDA (AI capex bet) at 12% and TLT (rate cut bet) at 8%, but AI capex boom historically coincides with higher-for-longer rates. These positions pull in opposite macro directions." No advisor has a tool that tells them this today.


What it solves: Portfolio coherence. The advisor's most painful blind spot. Surfaces what every CIO knows but no accessible tool computes.

Depends on: Existing portfolio_snapshot + SEC sector data + market agent (already built). Adds a new @portfolio-coherence agent.

User payoff: Direct answer to "is my portfolio internally consistent?" — the question every client asks and every advisor struggles to answer systematically.

My read

A (Conviction Ledger) is the foundation that makes B more defensible later. If RAPHI tracks its own accuracy first, then when the Portfolio Narrative agent makes a claim, it can say "this contradiction pattern has historically mattered — here's RAPHI's track record on similar calls." But B has more immediate user impact and is fully buildable on the current stack. Either order works.
